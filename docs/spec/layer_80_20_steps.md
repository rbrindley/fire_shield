# Fire Shield — 80/20 Actions by Layer

**Principle:** These are the highest-impact actions per layer, based on fire science evidence — not an arbitrary count. Some layers have three actions, some have five. The number reflects where the evidence shows the real leverage is, not a formatting choice.

**Governing fact:** Up to 90% of homes destroyed in wildfires are ignited by wind-blown embers, not direct flame contact (IBHS). Embers can travel miles ahead of the fire front. This means the 80/20 is overwhelmingly weighted toward ember defense — sealing entry points, eliminating fuel near the structure, and removing debris that gives embers something to ignite when they land.

---

## Layer 0 — The House Itself

This is the target. Every other layer exists to protect this one. Layer 0 includes both structural hardening and household readiness, because a hardened house means nothing if the people inside it can't get out safely.

### Why this layer matters most
Embers don't need the fire to reach your house. They land on your roof, collect in your gutters, slip through your vents, and blow under your garage door. The house ignites from the inside out before flames are anywhere near it. IBHS testing showed that when the wildfire-resistant side of a test duplex was exposed to an ember storm, it didn't burn — proving that if embers lack fuel and entry points, the house survives.

### 80/20 Actions

**1. Screen all vents with 1/8-inch noncombustible metal mesh or install ember-resistant vents.**
Attic vents, crawlspace vents, soffit vents, gable vents — every one is an ember highway into your home. Embers enter, ignite insulation or stored materials, and the house burns from the inside out with no exterior damage visible until it's too late. This is the single highest-impact structural action.
- Evidence: IBHS Wildfire Prepared Home standard lists vent protection as a core requirement. CAL FIRE and NFPA guidance are unanimous. Homes in the 2025 LA fires with four hardening features (including vent screening) survived 54% vs 36% with only one feature.
- Effort: Low-moderate. Mesh screening is a weekend DIY project. Ember-resistant vent replacement is moderate cost ($15–50 per vent).

**2. Clean all debris from roof, gutters, and downspouts — and keep them clean seasonally.**
Dry leaves, pine needles, and moss in gutters are a primary ember ignition point. Burning gutter debris gets under the roof covering and ignites the underlayment. Even a metal roof fails if the gutter debris beneath its edge catches fire.
- Evidence: IBHS research photos from the LA fires show gutter debris as a direct ignition pathway. CAL FIRE identifies this as the most common preventable ignition source.
- Effort: Zero cost. Requires a ladder and 1–2 hours, repeated seasonally (critical before fire season: May/June in Southern Oregon).

**3. Seal gaps around the garage door, windows, doors, and eaves with weatherstripping or noncombustible caulk.**
Every gap is an ember entry point. Garage doors are especially vulnerable — embers blow under the door and ignite stored materials, vehicles, or the structure itself. Open or poorly sealed eaves trap embers and radiant heat.
- Evidence: CAL FIRE Home Hardening guidance identifies garage door weatherstripping and gap sealing as priority actions. IBHS WPH standard requires it.
- Effort: Low cost ($10–30 in weatherstripping), 1–2 hours.

**4. Have a written, practiced household evacuation plan.**
Two evacuation routes (critical in the Rogue Valley where canyon geography limits options). A documented meeting point. Who drives which vehicle with which people and pets. Where critical documents are stored (or better, digitized and backed up). Who are your neighbors who may need help evacuating? The Almeda Fire showed what happens when people don't have this — communication systems failed and many received no advance notification.
- Evidence: Almeda Fire after-action reports. The Lahaina fire showed that even communities with plans failed when the plan wasn't practiced or updated. Ready, Set, Go! framework.
- Effort: Zero cost. One hour to create, practiced once per year.

**5. Prepare a go-bag that is ready to grab in under 5 minutes.**
Medications, documents (or copies), phone chargers, cash, change of clothes, pet supplies, water. Stored by the door you'd exit through. If you have to think about what to grab, you've already lost critical evacuation time.
- Evidence: Every fire agency in the country recommends this. The Camp Fire gave Paradise residents as little as 15 minutes to evacuate.
- Effort: Zero cost (you already own the items), one hour to assemble, stored permanently.

## Layer 1 — 0 to 5 Feet (Immediate / Noncombustible Zone)

This is the ember landing zone. When embers arrive — and in a fire event, thousands arrive simultaneously — they need fuel to ignite. The 0–5 foot zone determines whether embers that land near your house find something to burn or fizzle out on gravel.

### Why this layer is critical
Berkeley FireSafe research found that clearing the first five feet is the single most important exterior step to prevent ignition. IBHS found that when Zone Zero fuel coverage exceeded 25%, damage risk jumped to nearly 90%. Your fire inspector was exactly right — dead leaves under a deck are invisible to casual inspection but are the precise fuel that embers exploit.

### 80/20 Actions

**1. Clear ALL debris from under decks, porches, stairs, and raised structures.**
Dead leaves, pine needles, cobwebs, stored items, firewood — everything. IBHS research shows deck ignition typically occurs from ember accumulation in gaps between deck boards at joist crossings, where debris collects underneath. Embers fall through the gaps, land on the debris pile below, and ignite the underside of the deck. The deck then ignites the house. Your property could look perfect from the street and fail from a single pile of leaves under the back deck.
- Evidence: IBHS deck vulnerability research. IBHS post-fire photos from LA fires specifically document this ignition pathway. Richard's fire inspector flagged exactly this — a property with perfect plants and structure that was highly vulnerable because of hidden debris.
- Effort: Zero cost, 30 minutes, repeat monthly during leaf drop and before fire season.

**2. Remove all combustible materials from the 0–5 foot perimeter.**
No bark mulch. No firewood stacks. No propane tanks. No wooden patio furniture. No wicker baskets. No doormats (especially coir/natural fiber). No dried flower arrangements. No newspapers or recycling bins. During fire weather events, move combustible items indoors — a single burning doormat can ignite a front door.
- Evidence: IBHS WPH standard prohibits combustible materials in Zone Zero. IBHS post-fire documentation shows combustible items (hot tubs, furniture, planters) igniting and spreading fire to structures.
- Effort: Zero cost for removal. Low cost for replacing items with noncombustible alternatives.

**3. Replace organic mulch and ground cover with gravel, rock, decorative stone, or concrete within 5 feet.**
IBHS testing demonstrated fire spreading through combustible wood mulch and stopping when it reached noncombustible rock landscaping, preventing flames from reaching the home. Bark mulch within 5 feet of the house is a fuse.
- Evidence: IBHS research center testing. NFPA Firewise Zone Zero requirements. CAL FIRE defensible space guidance.
- Effort: Moderate cost ($100–400 depending on area), half-day project.

**4. Use noncombustible material for fencing, gates, and trellises where they attach to the house.**
A burning wooden fence acts as a direct fire pathway to your exterior wall. IBHS recommends noncombustible fencing within 5 feet of the structure, including where gates attach to the house. Metal, stone, or masonry for at least the first fence section.
- Evidence: IBHS post-fire documentation shows combustible fence sections attached to homes as a direct ignition pathway. WPH standard requires noncombustible fencing within 5 feet.
- Effort: Moderate cost. Can be done in stages — replace just the section nearest the house first.

**5. Enclose the underside of raised decks with 1/8-inch metal mesh or noncombustible skirting.**
If the deck is elevated, the open underside is an ember trap. Enclosing it with mesh blocks ember entry while allowing airflow. Do not use wooden lattice — it burns. Even with the underside enclosed, keep the area clear of debris (Action 1).
- Evidence: University of Nevada Extension guidance. IBHS deck research. CAL FIRE Ready for Wildfire deck guidance.
- Effort: Moderate cost ($50–200), half-day project.

## Layer 2 — 5 to 30 Feet (Intermediate / Lean, Clean, Green Zone)

This is the landscaping management zone. The goal isn't "no vegetation" — it's intentionally spaced, well-maintained, fire-resistant vegetation with no pathway for ground fire to climb into tree canopies and reach the roof.

### Why this layer matters
This zone is where landscaping choices either break the fire's progression or accelerate it. Ladder fuels — low vegetation that carries fire from the ground into tree canopies — are the mechanism that turns a survivable grass fire into a structure-threatening crown fire.

### 80/20 Actions

**1. Eliminate ladder fuels.**
Prune tree branches up to 6–10 feet from the ground. Remove shrubs, brush, and tall grass growing directly under trees. Remove any vegetation "staircase" that connects ground cover → shrubs → tree canopy → roof. This single action breaks the vertical fire pathway.
- Evidence: Every fire science source identifies ladder fuels as a primary structure-threat mechanism. Syphard et al. found the most effective defensible space treatment was in the 5–20 meter range.
- Effort: Low cost (hand tools), moderate labor (several hours for typical lot).

**2. Create horizontal spacing between plants and tree canopies.**
Trees should have at least 10 feet between canopies in this zone. Shrubs should be in small, isolated clusters, not continuous hedgerows. The goal is to break horizontal fuel continuity so fire can't race across a connected carpet of vegetation toward the house.
- Evidence: NFPA Firewise intermediate zone guidance. Spacing requirements increase on slopes — steeper slope requires more spacing.
- Effort: Low cost if removing plants; moderate if replanting with proper spacing.

**3. Remove all dead vegetation, dry leaf litter, and dead branches.**
Dead plants are the driest, most ignitable material in your yard. A single dead shrub within 30 feet of the house is a high-priority ember receptor. Regularly clear fallen leaves, pine needles, and dead branches from this zone — especially before and during fire season.
- Evidence: NIFC and NFPA defensible space guidance. CAL FIRE identifies dead vegetation removal as a top-priority seasonal action.
- Effort: Zero cost, 1–3 hours seasonally.

## Layer 3 — 30 to 100 Feet (Extended / Reduced Fuel Zone)

This zone transitions fire behavior. The goal is to interrupt the fire's path so flames stay small and on the ground by the time they reach your inner zones.

### 80/20 Actions

**1. Thin trees to reduce canopy density and remove dead or dying vegetation.**
Remove smaller conifers growing between mature trees. Reduce overall tree density so canopies don't form a continuous overhead fuel layer. Remove all dead standing trees (snags) and dead material. On slopes, spacing must increase — fire moves faster uphill, so fuel breaks need to be wider.
- Evidence: NFPA extended zone guidance. USFS defensible space research. Slope-adjusted spacing tables from CAL FIRE.
- Effort: Moderate to high (may require professional forestry work for larger properties).

**2. Clear vegetation and combustible storage around all outbuildings, sheds, propane tanks, and other structures in this zone.**
An unmitigated shed at 80 feet becomes a secondary ignition source. If it catches fire, it produces enough radiant heat and embers to threaten the house as if the wildfire were at close range. Apply Zone 1 principles (noncombustible perimeter, debris clearing) around any structure in this zone.
- Evidence: IBHS post-fire documentation shows outbuilding ignition as a common pathway to primary structure loss. NFPA guidance explicitly covers accessory structures.
- Effort: Low to moderate cost, half-day project per outbuilding.

## Layer 4 — 100+ Feet (Access and Community Zone)

This zone determines whether firefighters can reach you — and whether the broader landscape is channeling fire toward your property. Most relevant for rural Applegate Valley, Jacksonville foothills, and Ashland hillside properties.

### 80/20 Actions

**1. Ensure your driveway and access road are passable for fire engines.**
12-foot minimum width. 13.5-foot vertical clearance (trim overhanging branches). Turnaround space or pullout for large apparatus. If firefighters can't safely reach your property, they will bypass it.
- Evidence: NFPA access requirements. Fire department operational standards. Properties that are inaccessible are triaged out during active fire events.
- Effort: Low cost (trimming), potentially moderate if road widening is needed.

**2. Install reflective address signage visible in smoke and at night, and clear vegetation along the driveway corridor.**
Firefighters need to find you fast in zero-visibility conditions. A reflective, noncombustible address sign at the road and at any driveway forks is the difference between getting help and being bypassed. Clear vegetation 10 feet on each side of the driveway so it doesn't become a fire corridor.
- Evidence: Fire department access guidelines. Post-fire reports consistently cite inability to locate properties as a factor in protection decisions.
- Effort: Very low cost ($10–30 for signage, a few hours of clearing).

## Summary — Action Count by Layer

| Layer | Actions | Why This Count |
|-------|---------|---------------|
| Layer 0 — The House | 5 | The house itself is the target. Ember entry points, debris ignition sources, and evacuation readiness are all critical. Skipping any one of these leaves a gap that embers exploit. |
| Layer 1 — 0 to 5 ft | 5 | The noncombustible zone has the highest density of 80/20 actions because this is where embers land and need fuel to ignite. The deck debris insight alone justifies this depth. |
| Layer 2 — 5 to 30 ft | 3 | Three actions cover the core principle: break vertical fuel continuity, break horizontal fuel continuity, remove dead material. Landscaping choices matter, but the actions are simpler. |
| Layer 3 — 30 to 100 ft | 2 | Two actions cover thinning and outbuilding protection. This zone is about density management, not fine-grained actions. |
| Layer 4 — 100+ ft | 2 | Access and visibility. Binary — either firefighters can reach you or they can't. |

**Total: 17 actions.** Of these, 10 are zero-cost (labor only). 12 can be completed in a single weekend. The five most impactful are: vent screening, gutter/roof debris clearing, under-deck debris clearing, combustible material removal from 0–5 feet, and having an evacuation plan.

## Seasonal Urgency Calendar (Southern Oregon)

| Month | Priority Actions |
|-------|-----------------|
| March–April | Annual property walkthrough. Clear winter debris. Check vent screens for damage. Restock go-bag medications. Review evacuation plan. |
| May | Pre-fire-season deep clean: gutters, roof, under-deck. Replace any damaged mesh screens. Mow dry grass in Layers 2–3. |
| June | Fire season begins. Confirm go-bag readiness. Move combustible patio items to storage or indoors during red flag events. Final debris clearing. |
| July–September | Peak fire season. Weekly gutter/roof/deck debris checks. Monitor Watch Duty and NWS alerts. Keep windows closed during smoke events. Keep car fueled (evacuation readiness). |
| October | Fire season winds down (usually). Post-season assessment. Note any actions deferred during summer. |
| November–February | Off-season. Schedule any larger projects (vent replacement, deck enclosure, tree thinning, fence replacement). These are easier and cheaper to complete outside fire season. |

## How This Maps to Fire Shield

Each layer becomes a section of the zone-based map view. Clicking a zone shows these actions — not a generic list, but these specific, evidence-grounded, prioritized steps with citations. The count per zone varies because the evidence varies. The app should not pad Layer 4 to match Layer 0, and should not truncate Layer 0 to match Layer 4.

The seasonal calendar feeds the prioritization engine. In June, gutter cleaning ranks higher than tree thinning. In November, tree thinning is the project to schedule.

The citation system connects each action to its evidence source so the user can verify the recommendation. The "why this matters" text is not filler — it's the fire science storytelling that the research says actually motivates action (effectiveness framing, not fear framing).
